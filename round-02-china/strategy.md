### Alternative Strategies
- **Hadjar (S→H→H, 3-stop):** Went aggressive on softs early, but needed two hard stints. Mean pace 98.22s — significantly slower than one-stoppers.
- **Verstappen (S→H, 2-stop):** Started on softs from P8, attempted undercut. DNF lap 45 before strategy played out.
- **Colapinto (H→M):** Reverse strategy — started hard, finished on mediums. P10 result solid for Alpine's second car.
- **Lindblad (H→M→S):** Three-compound strategy. Aggressive but P12 finish suggests it didn't pay off.

### Safety Car Impact
Stroll's battery failure on lap 9 triggered Safety Car. This scrambled the field:
- Some pitted under SC (cheap stop), others stayed out
- Created a three-way fight for P2 behind Antonelli
- Russell eventually broke free; Hamilton vs Leclerc battled for P3

### Key Tyre Insight
- **Mercedes:** Lowest tyre degradation across both stints. Mean lap 96.46s (Antonelli) is absurd consistency.
- **Ferrari:** Higher deg on both compounds. Hamilton's last-lap 1:36.103 vs Antonelli's 1:36.929 — Ferrari faster at the end but only because Antonelli was managing.
- **The PU-deg connection:** Ferrari needs more power to run more downforce → less sliding → less tyre wear. Their chassis may actually be the best, but the PU deficit forces them to compensate aerodynamically, hurting tyres.

## DETAILED PACE ANALYSIS — Stint Chart Breakdown (Post-Race Data)

**Source:** F1 stint pace data — lap-by-lap comparison (Antonelli, Russell, Hamilton, Leclerc)

### Stint 1: Mediums (Laps 1–15)
- All four cars start in the 97-98s range
- **Mercedes (Antonelli/Russell):** Held constant or slightly improving pace throughout
- **Ferrari (Leclerc):** Clear upward trend — degradation visible from ~lap 8
- **Hamilton:** Between Mercedes and Leclerc — Ferrari chassis, better management than Leclerc
- Gap opens from near-equal to ~0.5s by pit window

### Stint 2: Hards (Laps 16–56)
- **Mercedes:** Run 96.5-97.0s consistently for 15 laps, then DROP to sub-96s on worn tyres
- **Leclerc:** Run 97.0-97.5s initially, then climb to 97.5-98.0s — classic degradation curve
- **By laps 36-42:** Mercedes gaining ~0.9 s/lap on Ferrari
- **Antonelli's final 10 laps:** 95.5-96.0s range on worn hard tyres — extraordinary
- **Mercedes laptimes NEVER trended upwards** across either stint

### The "Kings of Wear" Diagnosis
Mercedes had zero visible degradation across both compounds. Ferrari showed consistent degradation on both mediums and hards. The gap widened as stints progressed — the opposite of what you'd expect if it were a setup or fuel load difference.

**Root causes for Ferrari's higher wear:**
1. **In-team battles** — Hamilton vs Leclerc fighting cost both drivers tyre life
2. **Pushing harder in corners** — compensating for PU power deficit with mechanical grip
3. **Possibly less downforce** — lower PU output means less energy recovery, potentially running lower-drag config to keep straight-line speed competitive
4. **Car traits / setup** — Ferrari may stress rear tyres more inherently (T14 chicane loading)

### Fuel Load Context
2026 cars start with ~70 kg fuel (vs ~100 kg pre-2026). Empty vs full is worth ~2s, not ~3s. Despite this reduced fuel effect, Mercedes STILL improved pace through the hard stint. This means: the pace improvement was real car performance, not just fuel burn.

### Implication for Japan (Round 3 — Suzuka)
Suzuka is higher energy than Shanghai:
- 130R and Esses load tyres harder than any Shanghai corner
- Sustained high-speed sections amplify degradation differences
- If Mercedes owns tyre wear at a medium-severity circuit, Suzuka could produce an even larger gap
- **Ferrari's only hope:** cool conditions reducing thermal deg, or a setup breakthrough on rear tyre management

### 2015 Parallel
Two rounds in: Mercedes fastest, Ferrari clearly second, no one else close. The pattern matches 2015 exactly — PU advantage + aero platform + tyre management = uncatchable.
