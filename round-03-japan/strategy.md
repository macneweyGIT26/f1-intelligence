## Tyre Allocation — HARDEST SELECTION

| Compound | Color | Pirelli Code | Expected Performance |
|----------|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| Hard | White | C1 | Most durable compound available. Long stint backbone. Slower warm-up. |
| Medium | Yellow | C2 | Balanced. Expected race start compound. Lower deg than China's C4. |
| Soft | Red | C3 | Qualifying compound. Was the HARD at China — gives context on grip level. |

**Key insight:** China's **Hard (C3)** is Suzuka's **Soft**. This is a massive step harder. Teams will need to manage temperatures and warm-up carefully — these compounds take longer to reach operating window.

## China Strategy Lessons Applied to Suzuka

### What Worked in China
- **M→H one-stop** dominated (lap 15-17 pit window). 11+ drivers used it.
- Early pit window was unexpected — C4 medium degraded faster than predicted.
- Mercedes managed tyres leagues ahead of everyone.

### What Suzuka Changes
1. **Harder compounds = longer stints.** C1/C2/C3 at Suzuka means the pit window should be later (lap 20-28 range for a one-stop).
2. **Lower degradation overall.** But Suzuka's sustained high-speed corners load the left-side tyres heavily (Esses, 130R, Spoon all right-handers).
3. **Warm-up matters more.** C1 hard needs 2-3 laps to reach operating temperature. Teams that can switch it on earlier gain advantage.
4. **Track position is more critical.** Suzuka has fewer overtaking opportunities than Shanghai. Qualifying position and first-lap gains are more important.

## Expected Race Strategies

### Mercedes (Expected P1-P2)
- **Baseline:** C2 (Medium) start → C1 (Hard), one-stop around lap 22-26
- **Alternative:** C3 (Soft) start → C2 (Medium), if they want to control early pace
- **Note:** With their tyre management advantage, they could feasibly one-stop with any combination. Expect them to control from the front and pit based on gap management.

### Ferrari (Expected P3-P4)
- **Baseline:** C2 → C1, mirror Mercedes timing
- **Offset play:** C3 start → undercut attempt → C1 long final stint
- **Key variable:** Do harder compounds reduce their deg disadvantage? China showed +0.64s/lap gap. If Suzuka's harder rubber closes that to +0.4s, Ferrari becomes a genuine threat.

### Alpine/Haas (Best of the Rest)
- **Standard:** C2 → C1, one-stop
- **Bearman watch:** If he qualifies well again (China P10→P5), Haas might try aggressive strategy to maximise result.

### Red Bull (IF they finish)
- **Verstappen will try offset** — nothing to lose from likely P7-P10 grid position
- **Two-stop risk:** With C3/C2/C1 all available and lower deg, a two-stop is more viable at Suzuka's ~24s pit loss
- **Reliability is the bigger question** — strategy discussion is moot if the battery fails again

## Suzuka-Specific Strategy Notes

- **Pit lane loss:** ~24-26 seconds — higher than Shanghai's ~22s. Makes one-stop even more critical.
- **Safety car probability:** MEDIUM (~30%). Suzuka has tight walls at the Esses and chicane. New regs may increase incidents.
- **Weather risk:** Late March in Suzuka = changeable. Rain is possible. Mixed conditions historically favor Mercedes (better car = less risk in transitions).
- **DRS/Active Aero zones:** 2 (main straight, back straight). Effective but limited compared to circuits with 3 zones.
- **Traffic:** With 22 cars and 3.5s/lap pace spread, lapped traffic will be significant by lap 20-25. Leaders' traffic management matters.
